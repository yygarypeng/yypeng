Research

Research Direction

Recovering rest-frame information from missing-neutrino events

My research starts from a measurement challenge in $H \to WW^* \to \ell\nu\ell\nu$: the final state carries quantum-correlation and polarization information, but two neutrinos leave the event kinematics under-constrained. I study how much of that hidden structure can be reconstructed in a way that remains physically interpretable.

Scientific Problem

Incomplete events, testable observables

The common thread is to turn incomplete detector-level information into observables that can be validated, interpreted, and reviewed inside ATLAS. This requires reconstruction models that respect physics constraints, not just models that optimize a loss function on simulated truth labels.

Physics

Physics questions

  • What quantum-correlation information is still measurable in $H \to WW^* \to \ell\nu\ell\nu$?
  • How much W-rest-frame information can be recovered when two neutrinos are unobserved?
  • Which observables remain stable under realistic detector effects and systematic checks?
  • How can reconstruction tools be packaged so collaborators can inspect, reproduce, and reuse them?

Methods

Methods and tools

  • Reconstruction: transformers, normalizing flows, physics-constrained regression, ONNX export
  • HEP analysis: ROOT, FastFrames, uproot, mplhep
  • Collaboration software: Python, C++, UNIX, Git, Docker/Podman, CVMFS

Validation Practice

Useful only if it survives review

I prioritize reconstruction methods that remain interpretable, stress-tested, and usable in ATLAS review workflows. That means comparing outputs with physics expectations, documenting failure modes, preserving reproducibility, and keeping the analysis interface practical for collaborators.

Future Direction

From one difficult channel to reusable inference tools

The broader goal is to develop reconstruction and validation strategies that can transfer across final states with missing particles, detector limitations, or ambiguous object assignments. Higgs measurements provide the immediate physics target; reusable collaboration-ready inference tools provide the longer-term contribution.
